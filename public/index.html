In the nineteenth century, Uzbek women produced fabulous embroidered hangings, bed covers, wrapping cloths, table covers, and prayer mats for their households and their daughters' dowries. The decorative functional objects women made often spoke in a secret language bore a covert imagery. When we read these images in needlework, in paintings, in quilts, rugs, we sometimes find a cry for help, sometimes an allusion to a secret political alignment, sometimes a moving symbol about the relationships between men and women. Our family owned business – needle-work embroidery, machine embroidery, gold embroidery, traditionally goes back to the middle of 20th century, leaving footprints in every generation. Passing from generation to generation our family tries to keep the originality of patterns, making the fusion of ancient and modern styles.

Suzani

Suzanis are one of the most popular folk arts in Uzbekistan. Suzani not only means “needlework”, but also refers to the large panels covered with this decorative embroidery. These hand-embroidered vintage suzanis are infused with the character that only comes from everyday use. Suzani usually have a cotton (sometimes silk) fabric base, which is embroidered in silk or cotton thread. Chain, satin, and buttonhole stitches are the primary stitches used. There is also extensive use of couching, in which decorative thread laid on the fabric as a raised line is stitched in place with a second thread. Suzanis are often made in two or more pieces, that are then stitched together. Popular design motifs include sun and moon disks, flowers (especially tulips, carnations, and irises), leaves and vines, fruits (especially pomegranates), and occasional fish and birds.

Decoration homes with Suzani

Suzanis are still produced in large numbers in Uzbekistan, where they decorate homes, workplaces, teahouses and public buildings, and are still used at weddings and on festive occasions. The main centres of suzani embroidery are Samarkand, Urgut, Surkhandariya, Kashkadariya and the Bukhara regions, and in Shakhrisabz, Andijan and Tashkent. Embroidery produced in each region has its own distinguishing features.

Bouquets of Flowers

The abundance of suzanis depicting bouquets of flowers and the variety of flora displayed further demonstrate the prestige and importance given in this region to the art of the garden. Examples embroidered with floral sprays have almost always been erroneously attributed to the Nurata region, even though such motifs were clearly used throughout the western suzani-making region, in Kermina, Bukhara Samarkand, Shakhrisyabz, Ura-Tube and Tashkent. The composition is drawn in a more simplistic manner in examples we believe come from the lower end of the Zarafshan Valley, north of Bukhara and the Nurata Mountains. Some of the earliest examples attributable to the Samarkand region further up the Zarafshan River, in common with those from the Shakhrisyabz area, appear not only to be more complex in their drawing, but also to have a greater range of colours; in addition, they employ a greater number of embroidery techniques. Suzanis believed to originate from the Nurata Mountains are relatively subdued and use a smaller range of colours. Some versions from the Bukhara region tend to be bold and often rather rustic in design, while others have a delicacy of drawing and complexity of composition not often associated with this location.
